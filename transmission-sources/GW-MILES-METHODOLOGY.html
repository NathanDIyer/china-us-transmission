GW·Miles: New-Build 2010–2024

Miles counts wire. GW·miles counts power. One mile of 765kV carries 7.5× more than one mile of 230kV. This document derives the weighted-average capacity per mile for each country from official voltage-class data.

Why It Matters
Capacity, not just wire
China builds higher-voltage lines that carry more power per mile
New-build GW·mi = new-build miles × weighted-average thermal GW per mile

Each country's average GW/mi is derived from the actual voltage distribution of what was built — not assumed.

China: 3.52 GW/mi → ~1,216K GW·miles

Source: CEC Voltage-Class Data (2012–2019)

The China Electricity Council (中国电力企业联合会) publishes annual power statistics (电力统计年快报) with cumulative circuit-km by voltage class. English translations at chinaenergyportal.org for 2012–2019.

Voltage 2012 (km) 2019 (km) Added (km) Thermal GW GW·km
220kV AC 318,217 454,585 136,368 0.6 81,821
330kV AC 22,701 32,314 9,613 1.0 9,613
500kV AC 137,104 195,636 58,532 2.7 158,036
750kV AC 10,088 23,256 13,168 4.5 59,256
1000kV AC (UHV) 639 10,872 10,233 6.0 61,398
±800kV DC (UHV) 5,314 21,907 16,593 8.0 132,744
±1100kV DC (UHV) 0 3,295 3,295 12.0 39,540
Total 247,802 542,408
Weighted average: 542,408 ÷ 247,802 = 2.189 GW/km × 1.609 = 3.52 GW/mi

What Drives the Average

Even though 55% of new km is at 220kV (0.6 GW), the higher voltages dominate the GW·km:

Category% of km% of GW·km
220kV (0.6 GW)55.0%15.1%
330–750kV (1.0–4.5 GW)32.8%41.8%
UHV (6.0–12.0 GW)12.2%43.1%
UHV is 12% of the distance but 43% of the capacity. A single ±800kV DC corridor carries as much power as 13 parallel 220kV lines.

Gap: CEC Data Stops at 2019

Detailed voltage-class breakdowns are only available through 2019. After that, CEC publishes only aggregate 220kV+ totals. UHV construction continued — so how much does this matter?

Impact: UHV share dropped from 12.2% to ~7.4%, and per-km capacity dropped ~13% (more AC, fewer mega-DC lines like the 12 GW Changji–Guquan). Combined effect on overall average: <2% (7% share × 13% reduction ≈ 1% drag). Adjusting gives 3.47 GW/mi instead of 3.52. We use 3.52 as stated.

Post-2019 UHV Lines (from Wikipedia)

2020 (7 lines, ~4,710 route-km): Qinghai–Henan ±800kV DC (1,587 km, 8 GW), Wudongde–Guangxi–Guangdong ±800kV DC (1,489 km, 8 GW), plus 5 AC lines (~1,634 route-km).

2021 (3 lines, ~3,179 route-km): Yazhong–Jiangxi ±800kV DC (1,711 km, 8 GW), Shaanbei–Hubei ±800kV DC (1,127 km, 8 GW), Nanchang–Changsha 1000kV AC (341 route-km).

2022 (4 lines, ~4,513 route-km): Baihetan–Jiangsu ±800kV DC (2,087 km, 8 GW), Baihetan–Zhejiang ±800kV DC (2,193 km, 8 GW), plus 2 AC lines (~233 route-km).

2023 (2 lines, 525 route-km): Two short AC interconnectors (Wuhan–Zhumadian, Fuzhou–Xiamen).

2024 (3 lines, ~1,481 route-km): Sichuan–Chongqing 1000kV AC (658 route-km, 24 GW — highest-rated AC), Zhangbei–Shengli 1000kV AC (366 route-km), Wuhan–Nanchang 1000kV AC (457 route-km).

China Miles: 345,480

Dashboard sums chinaAnnual (15 values, 2010–2024). The canonical source chain: (1) chart_ready_data.annual_construction_km.china, (2) construction_rate_comparison_km_per_year (bridge for pre-2014), (3) annual_construction_rates.china (legacy fallback). 2014–2023 from Ditan Chart 5-14 additions (新增220千伏及以上输电线路长度). 2024 derived from CEC cumulative delta (960,970 − 920,500 = 40,470 km).

Sum: 345,480 miles (~556,000 km). This is ~3% higher than the CEC-implied ~538,000 km (960,970 end-2024 minus ~423,000 start-2010), primarily from pre-2014 bridge estimates.

345,480 mi × 3.52 GW/mi = 1,216,090 GW·mi ≈ 1,216K

US: 1.33 GW/mi → ~37K GW·miles

Source: td-voltage-aging.json (FERC Form 1 via PUDL)

FERC Form 1 Schedule 422 requires investor-owned utilities to report every transmission line segment with operating voltage and length. The PUDL project (Public Utility Data Liberation) extracts this into machine-readable form.

Computation: 2009 → 2024 endpoint comparison

Rather than summing noisy year-over-year changes (which suffer from utility reclassifications), we compare total system miles at start and end:

Voltage Class 2009 (mi) 2024 (mi) Net Change Thermal GW GW·mi
230–344 kV 38,344 51,708 +13,364 0.6 +8,019
345–499 kV 35,828 47,396 +11,568 1.5 +17,353
500–764 kV 15,518 19,789 +4,272 2.7 +11,533
765+ kV 2,370 2,975 +605 4.5 +2,723
Total +29,809 +39,628
Weighted average: 39,628 ÷ 29,809 = 1.329 GW/mi → rounded to 1.33

Note on mile totals: The td-voltage-aging endpoint gives 29,809 mi at 230kV+. The dashboard's usAnnual array sums to 27,890 mi (345kV+ from Grid Strategies + flat 891 mi/yr for 230–344kV). The ~1,920 mi gap (6.4%) reflects a known approximation; the 1.33 GW/mi average is robust regardless of which mile total is applied.

27,890 mi × 1.33 GW/mi = 37,094 GW·mi ≈ 37K

What Drives the Average

Category% of miles% of GW·mi

Nearly half the US net additions are at 230–344kV (0.6 GW). Only 605 miles of 765kV were added in 15 years. There is no US equivalent of China's UHV program.

Why 2009 Baseline (Not 2010)?

The dashboard covers construction during 2010–2024. The 2009 stock = what existed before 2010. Using 2010 would exclude 2010's construction.

Coverage Note

FERC Form 1 covers investor-owned utilities (~80% of US transmission). The voltage distribution of non-FERC utilities is likely similar, so 1.33 GW/mi is representative.

US HVDC Note

The US has no UHV lines. Existing HVDC: Pacific DC Intertie (±500kV, 3.1 GW, 846 mi, 1970) and Quebec–New England (±450kV, 2.0 GW, 930 mi, 1990). Both pre-2010. SunZia (±525kV, 3.0 GW, under construction) will be the first significant US HVDC in decades.

Thermal Capacity Ratings

Both countries use the same per-voltage thermal ratings:

VoltageThermal RatingBasis
220/230 kV AC0.6 GWStandard two-bundle ACSR, ~600 MW thermal limit
330 kV AC1.0 GW
345 kV AC1.5 GWStandard US EHV rating
500 kV AC2.7 GWFour-bundle conductor, ~2,700 MW
750/765 kV AC4.5 GWSix-bundle conductor, AEP-class
1000 kV AC (UHV)6.0 GWChinese standard, SIL ~5 GW + compensation
±800 kV DC (UHV)8.0 GWBipolar ±800kV, standard SGCC design
±1100 kV DC (UHV)12.0 GWChangji–Guquan design capacity
HVDC ≤500 kV2.5 GWAverage of existing US HVDC lines
Why thermal, not SIL? Thermal rating reflects the physical infrastructure built — conductor size, tower design, right-of-way width. It's what the line can carry. SIL is ~3× lower for AC and can be exceeded with compensation. The DOE Needs Study uses SIL; we use thermal. Both are defensible — the key is using the same method for both countries, which we do.
