The Growing Gap

In 2012, China's high-voltage network was 76% larger than America's. By 2024, it was 220% larger — and accelerating.

The UHV Revolution

China has deployed 44 ultra-high-voltage lines totaling 50,000+ km (31,000 miles) — with 8 more under construction — a technology class that doesn't exist in the United States. The world-record Changji-Guquan line transmits 12 GW at ±1,100kV DC across 3,293 km (CEC: 3,295 circuit-km in 2019).

The Investment Paradox

In 2023, the US and China each spent roughly $80–100 billion on grid infrastructure — the wires, towers, and substations that move electricity, not generators — per the IEA, using the same methodology for both. China built 18 times more new high-voltage wire. Measured in thermal GW·miles, the gap widens to 47:1. The money isn't the story. Where it goes is.

Why Does So Little US Investment Reach New Wires?

Permitting
5–15 years

Multi-jurisdictional review, landowner opposition, and environmental litigation routinely delay or kill projects before construction begins. China completes equivalent projects in 2–3 years. (IEA Grids 2023, p.9)

Structural Fragmentation
~3,000 utilities

No single entity can plan or fund a national backbone. Cost-allocation disputes between competing utilities have blocked major interstate lines for years. China operates through two state companies on 5-year build plans.

Reliability Crowds Out Expansion
90% reliability spend

FERC approves reliability-driven upgrades routinely. New interstate lines face contested multi-year proceedings. Both earn the same regulated return — only one reliably gets built. (Brattle Group via Grid Strategies)

Why Costs Diverge

Construction Labor — China
~$13K/yr

Power-sector construction labor (ERI estimate). PPP-adjusted gap narrows to ~2×, but nominal costs drive project budgets.

Construction Labor — US
~$51K/yr (3.8×)

BLS/FRED average for construction workers (CES2000000003). Prevailing wage requirements on federal projects add further.

Permitting Timeline — China
3–4 years

Approval to operation for major UHVDC. Changji–Guquan: approved 2015, operational 2019. State-directed land acquisition.

Permitting Timeline — US
10–17 years

SunZia: proposed 2008, under construction 2025 (17 years). TransWest: proposed 2007, permitted 2024. Each year adds carrying costs.

Supply Chain — China
Domestic + 30 UHV builds

Vertically integrated: SGCC manufactures UHV transformers and converters domestically. 30+ standardized projects drive learning-curve cost reductions.

Supply Chain — US
Global procurement, 18-mo lead

Large power transformers (LPTs) overwhelmingly imported — 18-month+ lead times (DOE). Zero domestic UHV manufacturing. Each project is one-off procurement.

Economies of Scale — China
9.8 km/day build rate

30+ standardized UHV corridors with dedicated construction corps. CCTV (Nov 2025): 9.8 km of UHV line per day during the 14th Five-Year Plan.

Economies of Scale — US
Zero UHV, each project unique

No ±800kV DC or 1000kV AC lines ever built. Highest operational: 765kV (1960s–80s). Every major project is effectively first-of-kind.

Why the Gap?

The divergence is structural, not just budgetary. China's centralized grid operators build against binding five-year plan targets. The US fragments authority across ~3,000 utilities and 50 state regulators.

Governance — China
2 state grid operators

SGCC (88% of territory) + China Southern Grid. National planning with binding provincial targets under 5-year plans.

Governance — US
~3,000 utilities

7 RTOs/ISOs, 50 state regulatory commissions, FERC at federal level. No single national transmission plan.

Permitting — China
2–3 years

Approval to operation for a major UHV line. State-directed land acquisition with minimal legal challenges.

Permitting — US
5–15+ years

Average for an interstate line. Requires negotiation with thousands of private landowners. Many projects die in permitting.

Technology — China
Global UHV leader

Developed and deployed 1,100kV DC (world record). Exports UHV technology to Brazil, Pakistan, and others.

Technology — US
No domestic UHV

Highest operational: 765kV AC (1960s–80s). No 800kV DC or 1000kV AC lines. Limited HVDC deployment.

Grid Topology — China
Long-distance UHV overlay

Point-to-point UHV corridors move power 1,000–2,000+ miles from western generation to eastern load centers.

Grid Topology — US
Three weak interconnections

Meshed AC evolved organically. Limited long-distance capacity. Congestion at seams between RTOs.

Methodology & Data Quality Notes · Full GW·mi methodology →

Definitions: China "transmission" = 220kV and above (standard Chinese definition). US "transmission" = 230kV and above. These thresholds are comparable but not identical.

Cumulative totals: China 220kV+ network: Complete 2014–2023 annual time series from China Energy Big Data Report 2024 (中国能源大数据报告2024), Chart 5-14: 578k (2014) → 608k → 646k → 686k → 725k → 759k → 812k → 843k → 882k → 920.5k km (2023). These are CEC's published 220kV+ aggregate figures (万千米 units). Exact 2023: 92.05万千米 = 920,500 km (report text p.60). 2024 confirmed at 960,970 km per CEC Annual Development Report 2025 (中国电力行业年度发展报告2025, Jul 2025: "220千伏及以上输电线路长度960970千米，同比增长3.5%"). 35kV+ total: 2,477,000 km in 2024 (+1.7% YoY). Note: earlier dataset versions used computed sums of individual voltage classes from China Energy Portal, which undercounted by 5–8% vs CEC's published aggregates; the Ditan chart values are authoritative. IEA Grids 2023 (p.17) shows China total transmission at ~1.9M km including lower voltages; p.28 notes "710,000 km built in past decade." US total at 230kV+ (~185,000 miles) from EIA Electric Power Annual. IEA (p.18) confirms US saw ~3% transmission expansion over the past decade.

Annual construction: US bars show new 230kV+ circuit-miles. The 345kV+ component comes from Grid Strategies Figure 1 (visual estimates from FERC data, 2010–2022) and FERC Energy Infrastructure Update (2023: ~450 mi revised, 2024: 888 mi confirmed). The 230–344kV component is a flat +891 mi/yr annual average from FERC Form 1 via PUDL (13,364 mi net over 2010–2024 at 230–344kV). 2012 peak reflects Texas CREZ 345kV completions. Voltage thresholds are now comparable: China 220kV+ vs US 230kV+.

China source priority (important): The dashboard uses `chart_ready_data.annual_construction_km.china` as the canonical annual series: 2014–2023 from Ditan Report 2024 Chart 5-14 additions (新增), plus 2024 derived from official cumulative totals (960,970 - 920,500 = 40,470 km, rounded 41,000). 2010–2013 are bridge estimates from `construction_rate_comparison_km_per_year`. We intentionally do not use `annual_construction_rates.china` for charting because that legacy block mixes older computed values (2013–2021) with estimated years (2022–2024), which can drift from revised CEC/Ditan aggregates.

DOE Needs Study (p.iii) reports US averaged 3,300 circuit-miles/year at ≥100kV (2011-2020), a broader category.

GW·miles: All GW·mile figures use thermal ratings for AC lines and design capacity for HVDC. Thermal rating reflects the physical infrastructure built (conductor, towers, right-of-way) — the maximum power the line can carry. SIL (surge impedance loading) is a lower theoretical baseline that can be exceeded at any time with series compensation, SVCs, or STATCOMs. For reference, DOE Needs Study 2023 (Table VI-3, p.123) reports the US system at 85.6 TW-mi using SIL; our HIFLD-derived thermal total is 243 TW-mi (~2.8× higher).
AC thermal ratings used: 220/230kV≈0.6GW, 330kV≈1.0GW, 345kV≈1.5GW, 500kV≈2.7GW, 750/765kV≈4.5GW, 1000kV AC≈6.0GW. HVDC design capacity: ±800kV≈8.0GW, ±1100kV=12.0GW, HVDC≤500kV≈2.5GW.
Voltage breakdown chart (total system): US miles computed from HIFLD geospatial data — 52,244 transmission line features, haversine-computed from coordinates, totaling 186,623 miles at 230kV+ (includes both "In Service" and "Not Available" status features). China miles extrapolated from 2019 CEC voltage-class data to 2024 aggregates.
Hero card (cumulative build 2010–24): New-build GW·mi = cumulative new-build miles × weighted-average thermal GW/mi. China avg: 3.52 GW/mi (from CEC 2012–2019 voltage-class additions: 542,408 GW·km / 247,802 km = 2.19 GW/km × 1.609). US avg: 1.33 GW/mi (from td-voltage-aging.json / FERC Form 1 via PUDL, 2009→2024 net by voltage class: 230–344kV +13,364mi×0.6 + 345–499kV +11,568mi×1.5 + 500–764kV +4,272mi×2.7 + 765kV+ +605mi×4.5 = 39,628 GW·mi / 29,809 mi). US annual bars include all 230kV+ construction (345kV+ from Grid Strategies + 891 mi/yr average for 230–344kV from FERC Form 1). The GW·mi ratio exceeds the miles ratio because China's new build skews to higher-capacity voltages (500kV, UHV).

Investment: Top-line 2023 totals from IEA World Energy Investment 2024 (p.78–80): China ~$80B, US ~$100B. IEA tracks "power grid infrastructure" from T&D utility financial statements (p.78 chart title) — same methodology for both countries. Battery storage is tracked separately by IEA (p.79): ~$15B for the US, ~$11B for China in 2023; these figures are not included in the $100B/$80B above.
US breakdown: EIA/FERC Form 1 reports $27.7B transmission + $50.9B distribution = $78.6B for investor-owned utilities (IOUs). The ~$21B gap to the IEA total reflects non-FERC utilities — municipal utilities, cooperatives, and federal power agencies not required to file Form 1. Reconciliation: FERC IOUs ($79B) + non-FERC utilities (~$21B) ≈ $100B (IEA grid infrastructure). Battery storage (~$15B US) is additional, tracked separately. Grid Strategies (p.3) cites Brattle Group for ~$25B/yr in transmission-specific investment; (p.7) notes 90% driven by reliability upgrades. Pre-2023 IEA figures estimated from chart trends (p.78). China 2024 ($85B) reflects SGCC's announced record investment of 608B CNY. China grid investment in CNY (complete 2014–2024 from Ditan report Table 1-4, sourcing NBS; 2024 from CEC Report 2025): 412B (2014), 464B (2015), 543B (2016, peak), 534B (2017), 537B (2018), 486B (2019), 490B (2020), 495B (2021), 501B (2022), 528B (2023), 608B (2024, record). Generation investment first exceeded grid in 2020; by 2024 generation was 2× grid (1,209B vs 608B). SGCC plans 650B CNY (~$89B) for 2025.

Voltage breakdown: China voltage-class mileage 2012–2019 from official CEC data (China Energy Portal): e.g., 2019 = 454,585 km at 220kV, 32,314 km at 330kV, 195,636 km at 500kV, 23,256 km at 750kV, 10,872 km at 1000kV AC + DC totals. US projected-lines reconciliation by voltage class uses three inputs: NERC 2024 Table L, Our Grid Future project-level database (Feb 2026; proposed set excludes Complete/Terminated), and ISO/RTO class-specific disclosures where voltage is explicit (MISO 345/765, NYISO CHPE 320, PJM/SPP 765). To prevent double counting across overlapping datasets, unified class totals are computed as max(NERC, OGF, ISO) per class (lower-bound union). Reconciled result: ~35,367 miles at 200kV+ (vs NERC floor 15,099; OGF proposed 34,458; ISO class-specific disclosures 9,593). Full trace tables are saved in the repo under transmission-sources/ourgridfuture/extracted/, including reconciled_us_projected_miles_source_trace.csv and reconciled_us_projected_miles_methodology.md.
